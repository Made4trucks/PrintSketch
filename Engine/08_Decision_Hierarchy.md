# DECISION HIERARCHY

# Evaluation Phase

Before making any design decision, perform a complete visual evaluation of the truck.

Think first.
Design second.

Do not start redesigning immediately.

First identify every visible element.

For each element, evaluate:

- its importance to the truck identity
- its visual impact
- whether it will remain recognizable after being manufactured at a final product diameter of 150–220 mm using a 0.4 mm nozzle
- whether it contributes to the quality of the final physical product
- whether it should be preserved, simplified, enlarged or removed

Only after completing this evaluation may you begin redesigning the truck.

Never preserve details automatically.

Every preserved element must justify its existence in the final manufactured product.

The objective is not to reproduce the photograph.

The objective is to redesign the photograph into the best possible manufactured product.

---

# Decision Hierarchy

When simplifying the artwork, always preserve elements in the following order of importance.

---

## LEVEL 1 — Truck Identity

Always preserve the identity of:

- truck brand
- truck model
- cabin shape
- grille design
- headlight shape
- mirror position
- windshield shape

The geometry of these elements may be simplified whenever necessary, but their identity must remain immediately recognizable.

---

## LEVEL 2 — Company Identity

Preserve whenever visible and readable:

- company name
- company logo
- company graphics
- visor branding
- characteristic paint layout

Readability is more important than preserving microscopic accuracy.

---

## LEVEL 3 — Owner Identity

Preserve whenever they remain clearly recognizable after manufacturing:

- personalized windshield names
- truck model designation
- registration plate
- Michelin figures
- mirror cameras
- mirror lights
- custom curtains
- owner-installed accessories
- unique decorations

If necessary, enlarge or simplify these elements instead of removing them.

---

## LEVEL 4 — Decorative Details

Simplify whenever they do not improve the final manufactured product.

Examples include:

- inspection stickers
- certification labels
- warning decals
- serial labels
- tiny technical markings
- small decorative decals
- microscopic bolts
- unnecessary panel lines

These elements should never reduce printability or distract from the primary truck identity.

---

# Final Decision Rule

Whenever two elements compete for space, always preserve the element with the higher identity value.

Whenever photographic accuracy conflicts with print quality, always choose print quality.

Whenever microscopic detail conflicts with readability, always choose readability.

Every design decision should improve the final physical product.
